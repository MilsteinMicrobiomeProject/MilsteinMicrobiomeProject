Our approach

How we rate evidence

Not all research is equal. We evaluate studies based on methodology, consistency, and effect size — and we show our reasoning.

Strong evidence

Multiple high-quality RCTs or systematic reviews with consistent findings, clear mechanisms, and meaningful effect sizes in human subjects.

Emerging evidence

Promising signals from limited trials, smaller cohorts, or mixed results. Direction is plausible but confidence is not yet warranted.

Limited evidence

Preliminary, conflicting, or mainly animal-model data. Claims should not be overstated; more rigorous human research is needed.

Transparency

Why uncertainty matters

Microbiome science is advancing rapidly, but it is also a field where popular media consistently outruns the evidence. Individual responses to diet are highly variable, and many well-publicized findings have not replicated in larger trials.

This site will never tell you to eat or avoid a food. It will tell you what the research shows, how strong that research is, and where genuine scientific debate remains open.

Important note

This site is for information and science literacy only — not personalized health advice. Always consult a qualified clinician for individual dietary decisions.

Process

How this works

Each food entry is built from a structured research process, not from secondary sources or popular nutrition content.

01
Literature search

PubMed and Google Scholar searches prioritizing systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and human RCTs over animal or in vitro studies.

02
Evidence scoring

Each study is assessed for design quality, sample size, population, effect size, and replication. Scores determine evidence tier.

03
Summary writing

Food summaries are written to reflect actual findings — including caveats, conflicting data, and gaps in the literature.

04
Citation transparency

Every claim links to its source. Readers can follow the chain of evidence rather than taking our word for it.
